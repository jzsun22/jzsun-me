CASE STUDY

Contribution Tracking Redesign

Replacing a four-tap handoff ceremony with a passive, ambient system. Zero new screens, zero new agents.

Product Consumer Multi-Agent Flutter Google ADK
Role
Founding Product Engineer
Period
Aug 2025 – May 2026
Stack
Flutter, FastAPI, Google ADK
Status
Spec complete; impl. in progress

In brief

I shipped a feature, then redesigned it from scratch a few months later with the team's blessing. The original feature, baton passing, asked co-parents to perform a four-tap handoff ceremony to log who was on duty. It worked. Adoption was low.

The replacement is passive: existing tracking events become the contribution record, a Sunday digest replaces the handoff, and the whole thing happens inside the chat surface that was already there. No new screens. No new agents. No new logging primitives for the user. Most of the work happened upstream of the surface.

The product and my role

JustGrow is a chat-first AI parenting companion built on a stack of agents (Google ADK) behind a Flutter frontend and Python backend. Parents log baby tracking events in plain language, get personalized bedtime stories, share albums with family, and increasingly use chat as the universal entry point for everything. The product runs on Flutter, FastAPI, and Google ADK, and reached 200+ signups and ~50 monthly active users on word-of-mouth alone.

The constraint that shapes every product decision: parents are usually holding a baby. One free hand at best. Attention measured in seconds.

I joined as the founding designer-engineer. My boss owned product direction and the major strategic calls; I owned execution end-to-end across UX, Flutter, and some backend pathes (Python and AI agent prompts). This case study covers one feature I owned during my nine months on the team, and it is one of the more interesting ones to tell.

What I got wrong with baton passing

The original feature looked like this. Tap an icon. Pick your partner. Write an optional handoff note. Tap "Send Handoff." The receiver gets a notification, opens the app, reads the summary, taps Accept or Decline. If accepted, a shift gets created and backdated to the request time.

Five-plus taps for a single status change. Both parents had to be engaged simultaneously. The receiver had two hours to respond before the handoff expired.

I designed and built the full feature: API routes, Flutter UI, conflict resolution for two simultaneous handoffs, decline-with-notes flow, animated overlay, haptic feedback. The shift model in the backend was the most complex thing in the codebase outside the agent layer. It worked, but adoption was low.

The diagnosis is not "the UI was too heavy." It is that I designed for the user as a value-holder rather than the user mid-task. Parents articulating the invisible-labor problem in calm reflection were the people we built for. Parents on two seconds of attention with a screaming baby were the people who actually had to use it. They share a name and they are different people. I read the aspirational undercurrent and missed the operational one, which is depletion.

Where the feature did land

Two App Store reviews specifically praised the handoff feature. A postpartum night nurse wrote that she recommends JustGrow to every family she works with because the handoff is "perfect for overnight care." A parent wrote that the handoff "saved our marriage" by ending daily morning arguments about who did what overnight. Both fit the values-vs-state framework rather than break it. A professional caregiver's operational state already includes shift documentation; for her, declaring state is not effort, it is the work. A household where memory disputes cause daily fights has an operational pain that exceeds the operational cost of four taps. The average parent's state does not match their value-holder self, but specific segments whose states do match are real.

The new design does not close those use cases off. Passive attribution still records every event with a user_id, so the data both reviewers valued is still captured automatically. They reach it through chat instead of a handoff form. The parent who was fighting about who fed the baby at 2am can ask "what happened overnight?" and get a summary. The night nurse can ask the agent to recap her shift and the parents see it when they wake up. One extra sentence to type instead of a four-tap ceremony, and the majority of parents who never wanted baton passing no longer pay for it.

The cost was concrete. The shift model carried more state and more transition logic than any other feature, and most of it became dead weight when we deprecated.

The redesign brief

The team made the call to deprecate baton passing. My job was the replacement.

The temptation was to iterate on the surface. Prefilled slots, better animation, a more discoverable entry point. That approach would have reproduced the same product mistake at a slightly lower cost.

The actual insight: the data about who did what already existed in the app. Every tracking event has a user_id. Every calendar event has an owner. The system did not need a handoff ceremony. It needed to read the data that was already being written and surface it intelligently.

Parents do things. They do not declare that they are doing things. Any product model that asks them to declare state will lose to one that infers it.

Design graveyard

Most of the work was ruling things out. I ran ten alternative directions to mockup or written-spec stage and rejected each for a specific reason. Every rejection was a product reason, not a resource one.

Several of these options came out of brainstorming with AI. The work is not the brainstorm. It is the rejection with a reason.

Killed

Care strip on home screen

A persistent card between app bar and chat. Stole ~80px of screen real estate permanently. The chat is the home screen and we do not eat into it. Idle state ambiguity: what does the strip show when nothing is happening?

Killed

Explicit "On Duty" / "Off Duty" model

Same bookkeeping problem as the handoff with fewer taps. Parents do not declare state. The tracking events themselves are the proof of who is doing what.

Killed

Bottom navigation tab

JustGrow is single-screen, chat-dominant. Tabs imply equal weight between sections. Chat is the primary interface by a wide margin and a tab bar would change the app's navigation paradigm to add complexity.

Killed

Per-person profile view

No existing foundation. Every stat the view would surface is answerable by a chat query, which is more flexible and has less UI to maintain. Building a screen on speculation does not pass the bar.

Killed

Dedicated Activity screen with three tabs

Log tab and Tasks tab duplicate Calendar. Only Contributions is genuinely new. A single tab does not justify a screen with two redundant ones.

Killed

Inline summary card in Calendar

Calendar is already dense. Same idle-state problem as the care strip. What does the card show on a day with no data? An empty bar feels broken.

Killed

Task assignment model

"Assigned to Dad, due tomorrow, status: pending" is Jira for families. Wrong emotional register. Creates a manager-worker dynamic between co-parents.

Killed

App bar pill (revived, killed again)

Risks repeating the original problem. Existing timer pills work because they are time-sensitive and disappear when the session ends. A persistent contribution pill has no urgency signal and would blend into the furniture.

The constraint that came out of the exploration was "zero new screens, zero new agents." It is not minimalism for its own sake. It is the call that fell out of every alternative being worse than reusing the chat surface JustGrow already uses for everything.

What replaced it

Three pieces, all in chat.

Passive attribution

Existing tracking events already carry user_id. No new logging primitives for the user. If they would not have logged it before, they do not log it now.

Past-tense logging via existing agents

"I went to get groceries at 2pm" said at 4pm currently falls to casual chat because it matches no tracking keyword and no calendar keyword. Two prompt expansions to existing agents close the gap; the backend already accepts past timestamps. Past-tense chore events are stored as calendar events with a source tag so the frontend filters them out of the calendar view; they exist only for contribution counting.

Weekly digest in chat

A Sunday cron generates a digest message and injects it into each user's chat stream as an assistant message. Two layers: a shared family-wide total with no names ("70 things handled this week") and a private mirror only the individual sees ("you logged 41 of 70"). The app provides both numbers. It never does the subtraction.

The "no subtraction" rule is the product boundary. JustGrow helps families see the work. It does not referee the argument. The product is built for collaborative families that lack visibility, not for adversarial ones. Designing for the adversarial case would make the product worse for the healthy majority.

The same restraint shapes how the feature shows up in someone's day. Participation is optional. There is no button on the home screen waiting to be tapped, no streak to keep alive, no pressure to log everything. The feature surfaces when you reach for it and stays quiet otherwise. And because contribution counts include any household work, not just baby care, the feature has reason to live well beyond the infant stage.

Sub-story: retiring the leaderboard

While I was redesigning contribution tracking, I argued for retiring the achievement system's leaderboard category at the same time.

Twelve achievements were live: "Top Caregiver," streak rankings, weekly winners. They were technically working. Users were earning them. The problem: in a two-parent family, "most active caregiver for 5 days" unavoidably reads as "you did more than your partner for 5 days." The easy fix was to keep the twelve and soften the titles. I argued that did not work.

Any positional criterion in a two-parent family is inherently a comparison. Soft titles do not change the math.

So the twelve were soft-deleted (existing earners kept their badges; nothing new awarded) and replaced with ten personal achievements in two new categories.

Consistent Contributor

Rewards showing up, not volume. A working parent logging once a day progresses equally to a stay-at-home parent logging twenty times.

What this work taught me

Five things I now hold more carefully because of this work.

01

Values-vs-state as a primary scoping question. The user articulating the problem and the user mid-task share a name and are different people. I name that gap before I scope a feature, not after.

02

Reuse over new construction. "No new screens, no new agents" was the call that fell out of every alternative being worse than reusing the existing chat surface. The constraint emerged from the work; every alternative I ran reinforced it.

03

Recognition over ranking, in any product where two users share an outcome. Positional criteria are a wedge in any product where the relationship between users matters more than the score. The leaderboard retirement is the small version of the same pattern.

04

Make intent legible. Every option I ruled out got a written reason next to it. The graveyard is there for future me, for teammates picking up the work, and for AI agents working in the codebase who would otherwise re-litigate calls that were already settled. The rejection note is the artifact, not the rejected mockup.

05

The default isn't always the right answer. Leaderboard, per-person profile, comparison view, task assignment model: the templated answers any brainstorm would surface first. The work was naming why each was wrong for this product. The discrimination is the same one I apply to catching AI-generated UI in other work.

Status

Diagnosis, design graveyard, product model, taxonomy, achievement redesign, and technical architecture are complete. Implementation is phased and in progress at the time of writing.

The mockups above are not Figma comps.
